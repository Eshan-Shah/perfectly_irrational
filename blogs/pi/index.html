Why π is a Weird Number

It’s irrational, transcendental, and it just doesn’t know when to stop.

By Eshan Shah · April 17, 2025

What is π, Really?

At its simplest, \( \pi \) is the ratio of a circle’s circumference to its diameter. But this innocent-looking constant turns out to be one of the most mysterious numbers in all of mathematics.

π is Irrational

You may know this already, but it’s worth emphasizing: \( \pi \) is **irrational**. This means it **cannot** be written as a fraction \( \frac{a}{b} \) where \( a \) and \( b \) are integers.

In fact, its decimal expansion goes on *forever* without repeating:
3.141592653589793238462643383279...

π is Transcendental

Being irrational is one thing, but π takes it further — it’s **transcendental**. That means it’s not just outside of fractions, it’s also not the root of *any* non-zero polynomial with rational coefficients.

There is no equation like \( ax^n + bx^{n-1} + \cdots + z = 0 \) that π satisfies. It simply floats beyond algebraic reach.

π Pops Up Everywhere

One of the strangest things about π is how it shows up in the most unexpected places — not just in geometry, but in probability, number theory, and even **complex analysis**.

For example, Euler’s famous identity:
\( e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0 \) ties together five of math’s most fundamental constants in one compact, beautiful expression.

A Weird Appearance in Probability

In the **Buffon’s Needle** problem, where you drop a needle on lined paper and calculate the probability it crosses a line — guess what? π shows up *again*.

The probability is given by:
\( P = \frac{2L}{\pi d} \) where \( L \) is the needle length and \( d \) is the distance between lines.

Conclusion

From the circle to infinity, from random needle tosses to the deepest corners of complex numbers — π is everywhere. And we’ve barely scratched the surface.

It’s irrational. It’s transcendental. It’s beautiful. And honestly, it’s a little weird. But that’s what makes it so wonderful.
